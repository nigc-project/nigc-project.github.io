Effect on Tribes

When asked for comment on the effects gaming has had on tribes, the National Indian Gaming Commission replied:

Revenue generated from Tribal gaming operations has helped to fund Tribal health and community service programs, law enforcement and education services. We have seen repeated instances where Tribes who have successfully operated gaming establishments have expanded over the years from a single facility to multiple operations across their lands.

The funds from gaming operations go directly to tribes. As described in the quote above, the revenue can be used to help strengthen tribal communities. Some of these tribes are isolated and therefore cannot easily engage with the economy, or have reservations on infertile land that can't be used to sell materials. Gaming offers a way for these communities to fund themselves and grow their economies, giving them access to better opportunities.

The total revenue brought in by Indian gaming nationwide in 2023 was $41.9 billion. This money goes straight toward the betterment of the tribes running the operations and creates a meaningful difference in quality of life.


An excellent example of this occuring is the case of the Seminole Nation. The Seminole Nation was one of the first to embrace gaming as a form of revenue, and, while their operations predated the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act and therefore encountered legal difficulties, it ended up being massively successful. The tribe wrote to a government official who had been attempting to shut down their bingo operations:

Between 1945 and 1977 an average of 4.3 tribal members per year graduated from high school. From 1978 to 1986, there were an average of thirteen high school graduates per year. From 1945 to 1977 nine tribal members received college or junior college degrees. From 1978 to 1986 twenty-eight members graduated from college or junior college, including one person who received a master's degree, and another who received a law degree. The Tribe now provides scholarships for worthy students, and of the twenty-seven identifiable special education programs available to tribal members, twenty are either wholly or partially funded by the Tribe.

Since 1982, the Tribe has funded a Seminole Cultural Education program which operates on all the residential reservations. The program, which utilizes bilingual University of Miami specialists and other experts, seeks to retain the cultural values, knowledge, and language of the Seminoles and heighten the awareness of the Seminole children to the uniqueness of their heritage.

A tribal newspaper, the Seminole Tribune, began publication in 1983 and is distributed to all tribal members as well as government offices, other Tribes, and various others interested in news of the Seminoles.

Finally, in 1981, the Seminole Police Department was created with law enforcement jurisdiction over the separate reservation areas. In the 1985-86 fiscal year, the Department employed 26 full time personnel, with the Tribe bearing approximately 85 percent of the total cost of the Department’s budget... the Department has been successful in apprehending non-Indian narcotics violators and, in the past two years, has confiscated two planes and several vehicles engaged in narcotics violations in Indian country.

Bingo revenues have been an important part of the Seminole Tribe’s ability to enhance and protect the lives of its members.

The tribe's ability to enhance and protect the lives of its members, in the end, is the reason why Indian gaming matters.
